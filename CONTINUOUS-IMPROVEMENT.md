# Continuous Improvement

Every repeated line of work should tighten the shell.

Directive-close loop:

1. verify receipts
2. check translation quality
3. record anti-patterns
4. propose one improvement
5. compact successful repetition into a playbook, operator, validated pattern, or regulation artifact

Weekly loop:

1. review completed work
2. score repeatability and quality
3. decide whether the line stays manual, should be white-labeled, or should be verticalized

## Why Improvement Is Constitutional

The shell is not trying to become static. It is trying to become more lawful under changing conditions.

Continuous improvement here means:

- compaction rather than accumulation
- explicit anti-pattern capture rather than quiet frustration
- better operator and playbook surfaces over time
- higher developer happiness through cleaner burden placement and less hidden state

## Three Improvement Destinations

Repeated work should usually end up in one of three places:

- **regulation** when the lesson is about what must or must not happen
- **quality management** when the lesson is about scoring, review, or control
- **automation** when the lesson is deterministic enough to become an operator, hook, or validator

If a repeated line of work never graduates toward one of those destinations, the shell is still leaking effort.

## Antifragile Criterion

The shell is improving when new work makes future work:

- easier to route
- easier to verify
- easier to resume
- easier to delete from transient context without losing state
