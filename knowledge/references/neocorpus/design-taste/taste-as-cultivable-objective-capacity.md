# Taste as an Objective, Cultivable Perceptual Capacity

---

## Core Thesis

Taste is not an innate aesthetic preference, culturally relative and immune to argument. It is an objective perceptual capacity -- analogous to athletic or musical skill -- that develops through deliberate exposure, improves with practice, and can be wrong. The "taste is subjective" consensus is a social truce, not a philosophical position. The practical refutation is simple: if taste were merely personal preference, there would be no way to get better at design, since everyone's preference would already be perfect by definition. Yet designers manifestly do get better. Their old tastes were not merely different -- they were worse.

The recipe for great work is very exacting taste, plus the ability to gratify it. Taste is the precondition for quality output, not an incidental byproduct.

---

## Key Frameworks

### 1. The Subjectivity Trap and Its Dissolution

The popular belief that "taste is subjective" arises not from philosophical conviction but from inarticulate preference -- people genuinely cannot explain why they like what they like, so they conclude that no explanation exists. This is an epistemological error: the inability to articulate criteria is evidence of underdeveloped taste, not evidence that criteria don't exist.

The cultural messaging reinforces this error from childhood. Parents suppress taste disputes with "you like to do it your way and he likes to do it his way" -- a social pacifier, not a metaphysical claim. Yet the same adults who tell children taste is arbitrary later instruct them to pay attention at museums because Leonardo is a great artist. The contradiction is unexamined. The child who absorbs both messages will conclude that greatness is an institutional designation, like a health food recommendation, rather than something demonstrable through comparative judgment.

Relativism is fashionable and actively hampers taste development: you cannot study what you have declared nonexistent. The productive move is to come out of the relativist closet -- admit, at minimum to yourself, that there is such a thing as good and bad design, and then examine how your taste has changed, what mistakes you made, and what others have learned.

The decisive logical proof: if taste were purely personal preference, improvement would be impossible. But designers do improve. Ergo, earlier taste was not different but inferior. Ergo, taste has an objective dimension.

### 2. The Philosophical Genealogy of Taste

Three thinkers from the 18th century mapped the terrain that modern discourse still inhabits, mostly unknowingly:

**David Hume -- Delicacy of Taste**: Taste has *resolution*. Just as a keen palate detects flavors a dull one misses, a developed aesthetic sensibility perceives nuances invisible to an undeveloped one. The analogy is wine tasting: some people genuinely perceive more. This is not a claim about superiority of person but of trained perceptual apparatus. The perceptual skill can be developed through deliberate practice.

**Edmund Burke -- Taste as Accumulated Exposure**: Taste is not innate superiority -- it is the record of what your perceptual apparatus has been exposed to and how it has been trained to respond. Your taste is the sum of your influences, processed through your nervous system. It is real and personal because it reflects genuinely unique experience; it is not arbitrary because different amounts and qualities of exposure produce demonstrably different perceptual resolution.

**Immanuel Kant -- The Subjective-Universal Paradox**: When someone makes an aesthetic judgment, they do not merely report a private sensation ("I find this pleasant"). They behave as though they are reporting an objective fact -- they expect others to agree, and they feel genuinely wronged when others don't. Aesthetic judgment is subjectively rooted but universally projected. This explains why taste disagreements feel so profound: they carry the emotional weight of factual disagreement even though they lack its philosophical status.

The synthesis across these three: taste is universal in its pre-verbal structure (everyone has it -- it is as structurally present as a pulse), personal in its particular calibration (shaped by unique exposure histories), and objective in that resolution of perceptual discrimination is genuinely measurable and improvable.

### 3. Taste as Neurological Construction

Taste is constructed from layers of exposure processed through unique brain networks integrating emotion, memory, reward, and personal meaning. It operates below the level of argument: you cannot intellectually convince someone to feel moved by something if their perceptual apparatus is not calibrated to register it. This is why taste transmission cannot work through principle-adoption alone -- copying someone's recommendations and taste judgments is like wearing their prescription glasses. The lenses belong to their eyes, not yours.

The critical implication for taste cultivation: you cannot shortcut to another person's taste. You must build your own through exposure. But the exposure must be guided -- undirected exposure produces unreflective habituation, not developed taste. Reflection on why you respond as you do, comparison against exemplars, study of what others have learned, and the willingness to acknowledge that your prior judgments were wrong -- these are the actual mechanisms of taste development.

A more productive response to radically different taste is curiosity: it provides information about another person's background, influences, and values. The logic of dismissing others' aesthetics as a "failure to have one" is consistent across cultural hierarchies but reveals more about the dismisser's limitations than the dismissed.

### 4. The Properties of Good Design

Practitioners across all disciplines -- mathematicians, scientists, engineers, musicians, architects, designers, writers, painters -- independently converge on the same recurring properties when describing excellent work. The convergence itself is evidence: if taste were arbitrary, independent traditions would not arrive at the same principles.

**Simplicity**: A shorter proof, a cleaner function, a building whose beauty depends on carefully chosen structural elements rather than ornamental profusion. Ornament is not intrinsically wrong -- but ornament as camouflage for weak structure is always wrong. When forced to be simple, you are forced to face the real problem. Elaboration is often evasion.

**Timelessness**: Fashions change; merit does not. To aim at timelessness is to aim at quality rather than trend-following. A heuristic: design for something that would also have appealed to people in 1500 -- both past and future periods are equally indifferent to present fashion. If it holds up across that span, its appeal derives from merit.

**Solving the Right Problem**: Much bad design is industrious but misguided -- enormous effort applied to the wrong question. The canonical example: a stove with four burners in a square, controlled by dials arranged in a row. The row is a simple answer to the wrong problem. Similarly, mid-century typography's war for "pure letterforms" via sans-serif fonts solved an aesthetic purity problem while degrading the actual problem -- legibility requires letters that are easy to tell apart, not pure.

**Suggestiveness**: Good design opens space rather than closing it. Jane Austen barely describes physical settings; her restraint allows readers to furnish the scene. Good architecture serves as backdrop for whatever life people want to lead. Good software gives users composable primitives. The suggestive work is more engaging because the reader or user is participating in its completion.

**Humor**: The very best work often has a slightly funny quality. Godel's incompleteness theorem seems like a practical joke. The Pantheon, the original Porsche 911, Bruegel's paintings -- they carry a lightness. Humor is related to strength: to have a sense of humor is to be strong, and the confident often seem to be making fun of the whole process slightly. It is hard to imagine something that could be called humorless also being good design.

**Difficulty**: Great work requires great effort. Hard problems call for ingenious solutions, and those tend to be interesting. When you have to climb a mountain you toss everything unnecessary out of your pack -- an architect on a difficult site or small budget is forced to produce an elegant design. Fashions and flourishes get knocked aside by the business of solving the problem at all. Wild animals are beautiful because they have hard lives. Not every kind of hard is good, though: the pain of running, not the pain of stepping on a nail.

**The Appearance of Ease**: Great designers, like great athletes, make it look easy. This is mostly illusion. The easy, conversational tone of good writing comes only on the eighth rewrite. Some Leonardo heads are just a few lines -- all you have to do is get eight or ten lines in the right place and you have made a beautiful portrait. But you have to get them in exactly the right place. What practice does is train the unconscious mind to handle tasks that used to require conscious thought.

**Symmetry**: Pervasive across fields despite periodic fashionable rejection. Two types: repetition (direct recurrence) and recursion (repetition within subelements, as in the vein pattern of a leaf). Recursion is especially powerful -- inductive proofs, recursive algorithms, structures like the Eiffel Tower. The danger is that repetition can substitute for thought: symmetry can be lazy as well as elegant.

**Resemblance to Nature**: Not because natural forms are intrinsically superior, but because nature's solutions have been optimized through evolutionary time pressure. When an engineering solution resembles what nature arrived at, that is a sign that the solution space is well-mapped. Copying from nature is not cheating -- working from life gives the mind something to chew on. The ellipsograph exemplifies this from the other direction: the instrument physically enacts the mathematical conditions that define the ellipse, achieving a consonance between structure and function that satisfies taste precisely because nature works the same way.

**Redesign**: Great work is rarely right the first time. Experts plan to throw things away. Cultivate dissatisfaction. Leonardo more or less invented the sketch as a way to make drawing bear a greater weight of exploration. The distinctive back of the Porsche 911 only appeared in the redesign of an awkward prototype. Make mistakes easy to acknowledge and easy to fix.

**Copying**: The development of taste goes through three stages. First: unknowing imitation (dangerous -- if you do not know where your ideas come from, you are probably imitating an imitator). Second: conscious originality. Third: selflessness -- the master wants to get the right answer, and if part of it was already discovered by someone else, that is no reason not to use it. The greatest masters are confident enough to copy without losing their vision.

**Strangeness**: The very best work often has an uncanny quality -- not merely beautiful but strangely beautiful. Euler's formula, Bruegel's Hunters in the Snow, the SR-71, Lisp. Strangeness cannot be cultivated directly; you cannot try to be strange. It emerges as a byproduct of trying to be true. Einstein did not try to make relativity strange -- he tried to make it accurate, and the truth turned out to be strange. The only style worth having is the one you cannot help. There is no shortcut -- no Northwest Passage. The path is through good and out the other side.

**Daring**: At any historical moment, prevailing orthodoxy contains propositions that are simply wrong and that people hold with violent conviction. Good design often requires challenging these. Ugliness is easier to perceive than beauty is to imagine. Most beautiful things got that way because someone saw something that offended them -- "I could do better than that." Cultivate intolerance for ugliness. Cultivate the voice that says "what a hack; there must be a better way."

### 5. The Community Amplifier

Individual taste develops in social context. Florence in the 15th century had Brunelleschi, Ghiberti, Donatello, Masaccio, Filippo Lippi, Fra Angelico, Verrocchio, Botticelli, Leonardo, and Michelangelo simultaneously -- a phenomenon that cannot be explained by genetics alone. Milan had comparable population and presumably comparable innate ability distributions but produced no comparable cluster. What Florence had was a community of talented people working on related problems, competing and collaborating, with shared standards of excellence.

Innate ability is necessary but insufficient. To produce a Leonardo, you need Florence in 1450. Modern equivalents -- the Bauhaus, the Manhattan Project, the New Yorker, Lockheed's Skunk Works, Xerox PARC -- produce disproportionate excellence for the same reason: concentrated talent, shared problems, and mutual standard-setting. Taste is sharpened by community precisely because community provides the exemplars, the friction, and the judgment that individual development cannot replicate alone.

At any given time there are a few hot topics and a few groups doing great work on them, and it is nearly impossible to do good work yourself if you are too far removed from one of these centers.

### 6. The Modern Ugliness Problem

Why has the modern built environment -- and much of modern design more broadly -- become so devoid of beauty? Efficiency has systematically defeated elegance. Cheap has replaced meaningful. The impulse to build things meant to last and meant to move people has eroded. Functional architecture may suppress the contemplative capacity in those who inhabit it.

Taste is not merely a personal cultivable skill but a cultural infrastructure -- and cultures can lose it structurally, not just individually, when their institutions systematically reward efficiency over beauty, speed over permanence, cost over meaning. The question of how taste survives -- or is restored -- at the civilizational level is distinct from the question of how an individual cultivates it. The two problems are related but not equivalent.

## Synthesis: The Cultivation Path

The cultivation path for taste has this structure:

1. **Accept objectivity**: Abandon relativism as a working posture. Admit that some things are better than others, and that your capacity to distinguish them is improvable.

2. **Accumulate exposure deliberately**: Taste is the record of accumulated exposure. The exposure must be high-quality, which requires curating inputs toward exemplary work rather than average work.

3. **Develop resolution through reflection**: Taste has resolution, and resolution is developed through the practice of attending carefully to why you respond as you do. This is not the same as generating post-hoc rationalizations; it is the cultivation of a perceptual vocabulary for the distinctions you are learning to make.

4. **Accept that earlier taste was worse**: The willingness to revise -- to throw away and redesign -- is not just a practical skill but a perceptual virtue. Cultivating dissatisfaction with your prior work is how you drive development.

5. **Go through good before reaching strange**: There is no shortcut to distinctive style or genuine strangeness. The path is: try to make good things, for long enough, until the voice saying "what a hack; there must be a better way" becomes trustworthy -- then follow it.

6. **Inhabit a community of people working on related problems**: Individual cultivation has a ceiling set by the quality of the exemplars and the sharpness of the judgment available. Community raises that ceiling.

The endpoint is not a fixed destination but an ongoing capacity -- a perceptual apparatus calibrated to register the difference between the well-solved and the mis-solved, between the beautiful and the merely fashionable, between the timeless and the trendy. The person who has cultivated taste does not primarily experience it as an opinion. They experience it as a perception: they see what is wrong with the same directness that they see color.
