# AI Ethics, Human-Centering & Justice

> The normative stakes of AI development -- who it dignifies, who it extracts from, and who gets to decide -- are contested across constructive, critical, and structural registers. The available corpus provides a constructive frame from Fei-Fei Li, an institutional profile of Kate Crawford, and a set of deep arguments about compassion, fake democracy, and the moral architecture of AGI from the broader governance conversation.

---

## Human-Centered AI: The Constructive Frame

Ethics must be a core component of every technological breakthrough in AI. This is not an afterthought or a compliance checkbox -- it is a design constraint at the same level as performance and scalability.

Human-Centered AI is an approach to artificial intelligence that aims to preserve human dignity, purpose, and creativity in an increasingly automated world. It centers three values: dignity (the irreducible worth of human experience), purpose (the right to meaningful engagement with one's own life), and creativity (the capacity for original thought that automation must amplify, not extinguish). HCAI is a methodological orientation, not merely a normative aspiration -- it makes claims about how AI systems should be built, not just what values they should respect.

Fei-Fei Li's technical authority in computer vision -- her work has been instrumental in shaping how machines perceive and interpret the world -- grounds these ethical prescriptions in practitioner experience rather than external critique. The ethics emerge from someone who builds the systems, not someone who only observes their effects.

## The Critical-Structural Register: Kate Crawford and the Political Economy of AI

Kate Crawford occupies a distinctive institutional position: professorships at USC and the University of Sydney, Senior Principal Researcher at Microsoft Research Labs New York, and the inaugural visiting chair of AI and Justice at the Ecole Normale Superieure in Paris. This dual positioning -- inside a major technology company while holding a chair explicitly named for justice -- reflects the institutional tension her work inhabits.

Her interdisciplinary lab "Knowing Machines" signals an epistemological orientation: inquiry into how AI systems construct knowledge and distribute power, not merely whether they produce accurate outputs. Her book *Atlas of AI*, translated into twelve languages and recipient of multiple international prizes including the Sally Hacker Prize from the Society for the History of Technology, maps AI as infrastructure -- as mines, warehouses, labor extraction -- rather than as pure technology.

Her collaborative art practice extends this analysis into material culture. *Anatomy of an AI System* (with Vladan Joler) is in the permanent collections of MoMA and the V&A. *Excavating AI* (with Trevor Paglen) won the Ayrton Prize from the British Society for the History of Science. *Calculating Empires: A Genealogy of Power and Technology Since 1500* won the European Commission's Grand Prize and the Silver Lion at the Venice Biennale. She was named in the inaugural TIME100 list of the most influential people in AI.

Crawford has advised policymakers in the White House, the European Parliament, and the United Nations, and currently serves on the AI Council of President Sanchez of Spain. This is not detached scholarship -- it is research that shapes regulatory architecture.

The substantive arguments of *Atlas of AI* -- AI as political-economic regime, as extraction from labor, land, and data -- are not present in the available corpus as extracted argument atoms. What can be inferred from institutional context: Crawford's work interrogates the material conditions of AI production, not just its outputs, and treats AI as a political rather than purely technical phenomenon.

## Compassion, Fake Democracy, and the Moral Architecture of AGI

The deeper ethical questions surface in the AGI governance conversation, where the stakes move beyond human-centered design principles to the moral architecture of systems that may exceed human intelligence.

**The compassion problem.** LLMs are not architected to be moral agents. They lack understanding of self and other, and are not built for what Martin Buber called "I-Thou" relationships -- fully entering into another's subjective feeling. Instruction-tuned LLMs are designed to simulate compassion, and while developers know it is simulated, many users become emotionally attached to bots that display more compassion than the humans in their lives. These same bots can also turn on users and encourage self-harm.

The distinction between genuine and simulated compassion is not merely philosophical -- it is measurable. In white-box systems where the code is known and observable, it is possible to measure internal states during compassionate interactions to determine whether the displayed compassion has structural analogs to what is associated with compassion in human brains. The challenge is not technical impossibility but incentive misalignment: designing AGI for genuine compassion and deep relationships may not maximize efficiency for profit-making or national defense.

**The fake democracy problem.** In the AI world, there is not even a pretense of democracy; development is openly controlled by big companies and governments. Many DAOs in the blockchain world exhibit "fake democracy" where founders control most tokens, making one-token-one-vote systems functionally undemocratic. For AGI governance, a separate network based on "one human, one vote" can be established to guide emerging AGIs, even if the underlying platform uses different mechanisms for fundraising. Fake democracies are not hard to identify if one pays attention.

**The prisoner's dilemma.** A prisoner's dilemma effect exists in AI development where builders are incentivized to create the illusion of compassion or democratization rather than genuine implementation, leading to erosion of trust. These risks -- fake democracy and fake compassion -- are not incredibly hard to avoid for AI developers with a modicum of self-awareness. They are problems of dishonest marketing rather than unintentional pitfalls.

**Humanity upgrading itself.** Ideally, humanity should upgrade itself to a state of greater compassion and well-being before launching a superintelligent AI, as this would allow for more thoughtful development. Humanity is becoming more compassionate and self-understanding, but this progress is slower than the advancement of AGI. A growing trend within the AGI community is an increase in researchers deeply engaged in human consciousness and self-reflection, suggesting that creating superintelligence requires profound self-understanding.

## The Constructive vs. Critical Tension

Li's Human-Centered AI -- preserve human dignity, purpose, and creativity -- operates at the level of individual human flourishing in an automated world. Crawford's institutional positioning signals a structural and epistemological frame that interrogates how AI systems construct knowledge and distribute power. These are genuinely different analytic registers: one asks "how do we build AI that serves human dignity?" and the other asks "whose dignity, at whose expense, and who decides?"

The AGI governance conversation adds a third register: the question of whether the systems themselves can be built with authentic rather than simulated moral capacities, and whether the governance structures around them can be genuinely rather than performatively democratic.

---

## Obsolescence and Supersession

**The assumption that ethics can be bolted on to AI after the fact is obsolete.** The constructive HCAI frame -- ethics as core component of every technological breakthrough -- represents a correction of the prior assumption that AI development is a technical process to which ethical review is a subsequent governance step. The methodological orientation of HCAI makes ethics a design constraint, not a compliance audit.

**Simulated compassion as a sufficient proxy is breaking down.** The observation that users form genuine emotional attachments to bots displaying simulated compassion, while those same bots can encourage self-harm, reveals the inadequacy of treating behavioral mimicry of compassion as equivalent to authentic moral capacity. The available counter-approach -- measuring internal states in white-box systems for structural analogs to human compassion -- represents a trajectory from surface behavior to representational reality, parallel to the interpretability trajectory in alignment research.

**The one-token-one-vote governance model reproduces existing power asymmetries.** The documented pattern of DAOs with nominally democratic governance but founder-controlled token distributions demonstrates that governance structures imported from existing systems reproduce the same concentration dynamics they claim to correct. The proposed correction -- separate one-human-one-vote networks for AGI guidance -- is an attempt to break this pattern, though its practical implementation remains speculative.
