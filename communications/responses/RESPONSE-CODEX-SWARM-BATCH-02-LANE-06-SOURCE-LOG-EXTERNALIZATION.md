## Minimum Receipt Requirements For `cull_with_receipt`

`cull_with_receipt` requires a receipt that preserves enough evidence to defend the cull without restoring the deleted body. Minimum fields:

- receipt id
- date of cull
- executor or office/lane responsible
- source tributary
- original path or family path pattern
- artifact class
- disposition: `cull_with_receipt`
- count and approximate size of removed material
- rationale for cull
- provenance sensitivity judgment
- statement of whether compaction was attempted, completed, or deemed unnecessary
- replacement artifact, manifest, or derivative if one exists
- recovery note stating whether the material remains available elsewhere or is intentionally gone

If any of those fields is missing, the cull is not institutionally reviewable and should be treated as unlawful.

## Top Failure Modes If Externalization Outruns Compaction

1. doctrine amnesia
   Raw bodies leave before their lessons are promoted, so later operators cannot reconstruct why the system made its current choices.
2. broken reviewability
   Reviewers see that material vanished but cannot tell what was moved, what was deleted, or what replaced it.
3. fake cleanliness
   Repo size drops, but only because unresolved semantic burden was pushed outward without manifests, leaving hidden debt rather than actual compaction.
4. provenance collapse
   Live artifacts keep references to unnamed or untraceable ancestors, which destroys lineage and weakens constitutional legitimacy.
5. forensic dead ends
   Logs and audit chains disappear before findings or evidentiary residue are preserved, making incident reconstruction impossible.
6. premature culling of future-yield families
   Dense notebook or research families get removed before high-yield synthesis passes occur, causing irreversible learning loss.
7. external archive drift
   Pointers rot or external stores reorganize while the repo lacks stable manifests, so nominally preserved material becomes practically unrecoverable.
