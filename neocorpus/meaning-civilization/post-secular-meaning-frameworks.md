# Post-Secular Meaning Frameworks

> The meaning crisis demands frameworks that operate between scientific materialism and religious tradition; biblical wisdom reframed through Jungian psychology, class structure as meaning architecture, and the attention economy as meaning destruction represent three entry points into post-secular sense-making.

## Biblical Wisdom as Psychological Architecture

Peterson's treatment of the Sermon on the Mount (02536.jsonl, 02538.md, 10004.md) reframes biblical wisdom through Jungian developmental psychology. The core move is extracting the psychological architecture from the theological packaging — not "God commands X" but "human flourishing requires X, and the tradition encoded this as divine command because the mechanism was not yet understood."

Key extractions: The Sermon on the Mount as an instruction set for meaning-generation under conditions of suffering. The beatitudes as a developmental sequence, not a list. "Blessed are the poor in spirit" as the starting condition for psychological growth — you must recognize what you lack before development is possible. The injunction against worry as a cognitive reframing: anxiety about the future prevents engagement with the present, which is where meaning is generated.

This sits in the post-secular space: neither accepting the theological frame uncritically nor discarding the accumulated wisdom it encodes. The question is whether the psychological architecture can be separated from the theological scaffolding without losing structural integrity.

## Class Structure as Meaning Architecture

The American class hierarchy analysis (01564.jsonl, 01566.md) reveals class not as an economic category but as a meaning architecture. The dual-pyramid model: economic class (income, wealth) and cultural class (taste, education, social capital) operate on different axes. People at the intersection — high cultural capital, low economic capital (adjunct professors, artists) or high economic capital, low cultural capital (new-money entrepreneurs) — experience acute meaning dissonance.

The coupling crisis (09872.md): digital segregation is producing parallel meaning-worlds that do not interact. People increasingly live in information environments that reinforce their existing meaning frameworks. The result is not disagreement but mutual unintelligibility — separate populations that cannot even formulate questions the other would recognize.

## Attention Ecology as Meaning Destruction

The ragebait economy (10272.md) represents the end of intelligence in a specific sense: the attention economy optimizes for engagement metrics that systematically destroy the conditions for meaning-generation. Rage, outrage, and tribal signaling produce high engagement but prevent the kind of sustained, curious, open attention that meaning requires (see Laukkonen's metacognitive gradient in the meaning crisis entry).

The attention economy is Moloch operating on cognition: locally rational choices (optimize for clicks) aggregate into collective catastrophe (destruction of the epistemic commons). This is meaning destruction at industrial scale.

## Tensions & Open Questions

- **The extraction problem:** Can wisdom be extracted from religious tradition without the tradition? Peterson's project assumes yes. But the tradition may be load-bearing — the community, ritual, and commitment structure may be inseparable from the content.
- **Class and meaning:** If class is a meaning architecture, does post-scarcity economics dissolve class or merely restructure it around new axes (attention, taste, agency)?
- **Attention as prerequisite:** If sustained attention is a precondition for meaning, and the attention economy systematically degrades attention, then the meaning crisis may be partly an attention crisis. This suggests that attention ecology interventions (not just philosophical frameworks) are required.
