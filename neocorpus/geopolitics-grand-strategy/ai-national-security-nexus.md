## AI as Civilizational Infrastructure

Three competing perspectives on AI's future: (1) extinction/superintelligence threat — AGI poses extinction-level risk; (2) total abundance — an era solving humanity's problems; (3) marketing hype — current enthusiasm is overblown. These frames coexist and compete for policy influence.

## Obsolescence and Supersession

**The "Moore's Law governs AI progression" assumption is superseded.** Wissner-Gross's claim (embedded in the sources) that Moore's Law is no longer applicable to AI progression — replaced by an experience curve — represents a specific supersession in how AI capability advancement is modeled. Moore's Law (transistor density doubling every ~18 months) governed AI capability predictions during the deep learning era partly because GPU compute was the binding constraint. The experience curve framing — capability advancing through cumulative exposure to problems across diverse fields — describes a regime where data and problem variety, not raw transistor count, govern capability development. The policy implications differ: export controls on chips (the dominant US national security tool for AI advantage) are well-suited to a Moore's Law regime; they are less decisive in an experience-curve regime where the binding constraint is problem diversity and data access rather than compute alone.

**OSINT democratization as a supersession of intelligence exclusivity.** The citizen-accessible intelligence tools documented in the sources — ADS-B Exchange, MarineTraffic, Sentinel satellite imagery, Google Earth Pro, LiveUAMap — represent a specific obsolescence of the assumption that open-source intelligence was categorically inferior to classified sources and accessible only to state actors. The prior assumption: real-time tracking of ships, aircraft, and conflict zones required classified satellite access or signals intelligence capabilities. The current reality: a citizen analyst can correlate ADS-B flight data, MarineTraffic vessel positions, and Sentinel imagery to reconstruct logistics chains that were previously visible only to state intelligence services. This does not replace classified intelligence, but it breaks the exclusivity assumption that made intelligence a pure state-actor advantage.

**Audit note:** These sources are relatively shallow on temporal argument chains — they document a moment in geopolitical-AI interaction without tracing prior assumptions explicitly. The supersession claims above are derivable from what the sources describe as current capabilities, read against what those capabilities replaced. Deeper enrichment would require sources that treat the pre-AI national security landscape explicitly.
